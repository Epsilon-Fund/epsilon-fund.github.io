## Why This Trade

Bitcoin has been grinding sideways for the past 48 hours with no meaningful directional resolution. This morning's spike to ~$69,700 reads as a stop-hunt rather than a structural breakout — a single flush of one-way buy flow that tagged the descending trendline on the 4H before stalling. The fundamental backdrop hasn't changed. We're selling the rip.

The 1H RSI is printing a textbook bearish divergence: price made a higher high on the morning spike while RSI failed to confirm, rolling over from ~75 into declining momentum. On the 4H, the structure is unambiguously bearish — lower highs, lower lows, and a descending trendline that has capped every rally since late March. The daily chart confirms the broader trend remains intact. This spike gave us a clean, low-risk entry point against a defined level.

## The Three-Pillar Thesis

**1. Bearish RSI divergence on the 1H — momentum doesn't confirm the move.** Price printed a higher high on the morning spike while 1H RSI diverged lower, rolling over from overbought territory (~75). This is a reliable exhaustion signal, particularly when it occurs at a technically significant level. The divergence tells us the spike was driven by mechanical stop-hunting rather than genuine buying pressure stepping in.

**2. 4H downtrend structure fully intact.** The descending trendline from the late-March highs has rejected price consistently across multiple touches. The morning spike tagged this trendline and reversed — providing the confluence needed for conviction. The 4H RSI at ~55 has room to roll over materially before reaching oversold conditions, meaning there is no structural momentum support propping this up on the higher timeframe.

**3. Sideways chop masks a bearish resolution.** The 48 hours of range-bound price action between roughly $67,500 and $69,700 initially reads as indecision, but the context is bearish: this consolidation is occurring beneath a declining trendline, inside a broader downtrend, with no fundamental catalyst for a sustained directional reversal. Ranges like this resolve in the direction of the prevailing trend. The morning's stop-hunt exhausts residual buy-side pressure and sets up the next leg down.

## Trade Structure

A single-leg short with staged profit-taking at two defined support levels.

**Entry:** ~$69,590 — the stop-hunt spike high / 4H trendline tag, confirmed by 1H RSI divergence.

**Take Profit 1 — $68,050:** The first meaningful support cluster visible on both the 1H and 4H charts. Take 50% of the position here to lock in gains and reduce risk on the remainder.

**Take Profit 2 — $66,500:** Long-term horizontal support that has acted as a key structural floor. Full exit here. This is the mean-reversion target — not a breakout trade.

**Stop Loss — $70,400:** Above the 4H descending trendline and the recent swing high at $70,283. A close above this level would invalidate the thesis structurally.

**Sizing:** 2% of portfolio. Risk/reward: 2.5:1.

## Risk Management

The stop is structural — placed above the trendline and swing high, not an arbitrary dollar amount. If price closes above $70,400 on the 4H, the thesis is wrong and we exit cleanly. No averaging down.

TP1 at $68,050 reduces position size by 50% and effectively makes the remainder of the trade risk-free relative to entry. If price stalls between $68,000 and $67,500 without clean continuation, we tighten the stop on the residual position to entry rather than ride back up.

The trade does not benefit from fundamental catalysts — it is a pure technical mean-reversion setup. Any macro or crypto-specific news event that drives a genuine bid above $70,400 is a clean stop and we move on.

## Trade Outcome

Closed April 7 at $67,827 — in profit, with price moving through the TP1 zone and into the lower support area. The thesis resolved correctly: the morning spike was indeed exhaustion, and the downtrend structure reasserted itself within 24 hours.

## Post-Trade Reflection

The trade worked, but the path was messier than anticipated — price came uncomfortably close to the $70,400 stop before reversing. That near-miss is worth examining carefully.

The core tension here is between stop placement logic and intraday volatility reality. The stop was placed correctly on a structural basis — above the trendline and swing high — but the market's behaviour around obvious technical levels is increasingly adversarial. Algos are clearly running stop-hunting strategies that probe beyond clean technical levels before reversing, and a structurally correct stop placed at the "obvious" location is precisely the stop that gets hunted.

Two things worth taking forward. First, consider calibrating stop distance to shorter timeframe volatility rather than anchoring purely to the higher timeframe structure. A stop sized to absorb 1H ATR rather than placed exactly at the 4H level gives the trade room to breathe through the mechanical sweeps without changing the invalidation logic. Second, be more selective about entry quality — the less obvious the setup reads to the crowd, the less likely the stop is sitting in a well-mapped liquidity pool. Setups that emerge from less telegraphed levels, or that are entered on a pullback rather than right at the trendline tag, will naturally sit further from the algos' targets. The thesis was right; the stop placement was too clean. That's the edge to sharpen next time.
