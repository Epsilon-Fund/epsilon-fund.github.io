## Why This Trade

WTI crude oil has **never in history** closed above $147.27 — the all-time record set in July 2008 during a perfect storm of peak China demand, speculative commodity flows, and global supply panic. The Polymarket contracts on oil hitting $175 and $200 by June 30 are implying 13% and 10% probability respectively. We believe fair value is 3-5% on $175 and 1-2% on $200. That mispricing is driven by three structural factors.

## The Three-Pillar Thesis

**1. Historical impossibility.** $175 and $200 WTI would require breaking the all-time record by 19% and 36% respectively. The chart below shows the full history of WTI going back to 2002 — the 2008 peak at $147.27 stands alone and was driven by a confluence of conditions that don't exist today. US shale production acts as a structural price ceiling above $100. Saudi Arabia and OPEC have both the capacity and political incentive to cap prices well below demand-destruction levels. The $55-65 level is where this market structurally wants to return once the conflict risk premium unwinds.

![WTI Crude Oil — Full Monthly History Since 2002]({{ '/assets/images/oil-wti-historical-chart.png' | relative_url }})

**2. Settlement mechanics the market doesn't understand.** This is not a one-touch contract. The Polymarket contract resolves based on the settlement price on June 30 — not an intraday spike, not a momentary print. Even if Trump follows through and oil briefly touches $130-140, it needs to be there at settlement. The bar is dramatically higher than most retail participants appreciate when they buy these tail Yes positions. This asymmetry in market understanding is a core part of the edge.

**3. Behavioral edge — we are on the opposite side of retail narrative flow.** Media coverage of the US-Iran conflict is generating intense FOMO among retail prediction market participants. "$200 oil" makes for a compelling headline and these markets attract unsophisticated capital chasing narrative momentum. Retail participants with no understanding of oil market structure, historical context, or settlement mechanics are piling into Yes on the extreme tails — inflating probabilities well beyond fair value. We take the other side of that flow systematically.
