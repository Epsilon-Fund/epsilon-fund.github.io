## Week In Review
The US and Iran agreed to a conditional two-week ceasefire, initially leading to a sharp decline in oil prices and a rally in global equities, with the S&P 500 gaining 3.58%. However, the truce remained uneasy, and sticky US inflation data, including a 3.3% YoY rise in headline CPI, tempered market gains and kept central banks on alert for potential rate adjustments.

## Geopolitics
A conditional two-week ceasefire was agreed between the US and Iran, with the US suspending attacks on Iran and Iran agreeing to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. This development initially led to a sharp fall in oil prices and a rally in global equities. However, the truce has been uneasy, with Israel continuing strikes in Lebanon and ship traffic through the Strait of Hormuz running at only ~10% of pre-conflict levels, indicating persistent geopolitical risks. Talks between the US and Iran are scheduled to take place in Pakistan, which may determine the path to lasting peace.

The Middle East conflict continues to impact energy markets, with Brent crude oil soaring to $99 a barrel from $72 earlier, and retail gas prices averaging $4.13 this month, up from $2.93 in February. Saudi Arabia reported a 600k barrels/day fall in production capacity due to Iranian attacks on energy facilities. Europe's energy security remains under strain, highlighting the challenge of cutting gas dependency.

## Inflation
### United States
US CPI inflation rose 0.9% MoM in March, with the annual rate increasing from 2.4% in February to 3.3%, primarily driven by a 10.9% increase in energy prices. Core CPI, excluding food and energy, was more contained at 0.2% MoM and 2.6% YoY. February's PCE inflation came in at 2.8% YoY, in line with consensus, while core PCE edged down from 3.1% in January to 3.0%. Higher gas prices are a concern, potentially offsetting the stimulus from larger income tax refunds.

### Europe
ECB policymakers are leaning toward keeping interest rates unchanged, and the March ECB minutes confirmed a hawkish pivot. Poland's March inflation increase narrowed to fuel prices, and Hungary experienced double-digit wage growth. Energy price pass-through remains a concern for the region given its dependence on Middle Eastern oil and the ongoing conflict.

## Growth
### United States
The US ISM Services PMI fell to 54.0 in March, below market expectations of 54.8 and February's 56.1 print, largely due to a 6.6-point drop in the employment index. Real GDP growth for Q4 2025 was revised down by 0.2 percentage points to 0.5% annualized, below the 0.7% consensus and Q3's 4.4%, primarily due to weaker consumer spending and a government shutdown. Consumer spending for the week rose 0.5% m/m. Despite these slowdowns, fiscal stimulus from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) and strong AI-related investment are providing an offset, with real equipment investment growing at a solid 10% annualized pace in Q1.

### Europe
Eurozone industrial production was sluggish ahead of the Middle East war. The Dutch economy is seeing recent momentum begin to fade, while the UK's growth surge "looks too good to be true" according to ING. China's economic growth rebounded, but its trade surplus plummeted as exports slowed and imports surged.

## Labour Market
### United States
The ISM Services PMI employment index fell by 6.6 points in March, reaching its lowest level since 2023, indicating a softening labor market. While March saw a stronger-than-expected net gain of 178,000 jobs, employment has barely increased year-over-year and shrank outside healthcare, with the decline in the unemployment rate to 4.3% driven by a contraction in the labor force. Average hourly earnings growth eased to around 3.5% year-over-year, and anecdotal recruiter data suggests a higher share of job switchers are accepting lower or similar pay.

### Europe
Double-digit wage growth made a cameo appearance in Hungary. No other major developments on eurozone or country-level labour data were explicitly reported this week.

## Central Banks & Interest Rates
### Federal Reserve
The minutes from the FOMC's March meeting indicated that "almost all" participants believe the Committee is "well positioned" at the current rate. "Most" participants suggested that further softening in labor market conditions "could warrant additional rate cuts," while "many" participants noted that elevated inflation caused by higher oil prices "could call for rate increases." The Fed Funds Effective Rate was 3.64% as of 4/10/2026.

### European Central Bank
ECB policymakers are leaning toward keeping interest rates unchanged, and the minutes from the March meeting confirmed a hawkish pivot. The ECB Depo Rate was 2.00% as of 4/10/2026. The market continues to price in at least two ECB rate hikes this year, as strains in the Middle East truce emerged.

### Bank of England
No major developments this week.

## Sell-Side Research Summary

**Goldman Sachs:** US equities rose on ceasefire news, driving the S&P 500 higher. Despite a slowdown in ISM Services PMI and Q4 2025 GDP, core inflation remains contained. GS believes the recent tech correction presents an opportunity, expecting strong returns driven by AI infrastructure investment.

**JPMorgan Asset Management:** Fiscal stimulus from the OBBBA and tax refunds are expected to boost growth, but rising energy prices due to the Middle East conflict could partially offset these benefits. While higher gas prices may pressure lower-income households, upper-income households are likely to fare best, and the energy shock alone is not expected to trigger a US recession.

**Bank of America:** The US economy faces a fragile equilibrium with soft hiring and moderating inflation, threatened by rising energy prices. However, fiscal support and strong AI-related capital spending provide an offset. BofA expects slower but positive growth, with the Fed likely to refocus on downside risks and resume policy easing later in the year.
