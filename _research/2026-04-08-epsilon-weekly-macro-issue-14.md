## Week In Review
Geopolitical tensions stemming from the U.S./Iran conflict significantly impacted markets, leading to a challenging month for both stocks and bonds in March, with the 60/40 portfolio declining 3.7%. Fears of higher near-term inflation from increased energy prices sent equity markets lower and yields higher. Despite this, US economic data showed resilience, with nonfarm payrolls rising by 178k and retail sales up 0.6% m/m.

## Geopolitics
Geopolitical tensions escalated this week, with Iran pressing on with attacks across the Persian Gulf and the US planning fresh attacks, leading to oil supply warnings and Saudi Arabia raising prices. This conflict has fueled fears of higher near-term inflation from increased energy prices, contributing to a challenging month for equity and bond markets.

Beyond the immediate conflict, the US faces significant dependencies on critical minerals, with China maintaining a dominant position in refining these essential resources for grid infrastructure, AI, and defense systems. The US remains 100% net import-reliant for 13 critical minerals. Efforts to reshore supply chains and protectionism around these resources are expected to be key themes, with public-private sector collaboration, including initiatives like "Project Vault," gaining momentum to address these vulnerabilities.

## Inflation
### United States
Fears of higher near-term inflation, driven by increased energy prices, have moved to the forefront, leading markets to price in no rate cuts for the remainder of 2026. Goods inflation in the US rose from 0.5% to 1.3% year-on-year in the 12 months to February 2026, partly attributed to tariffs. Policy uncertainty and ongoing trade tensions are expected to contribute to lingering inflation volatility. Headline CPI for March is projected at 2.4% y/y, with Core CPI at 2.5% y/y.

### Europe
ECB official Fabio Panetta noted that the impact of the war would persist, affecting inflation. Italy experienced a modest increase in inflation, though substantial risks remain. Eurozone CPI is noted at 2.5%.

## Growth
### United States
The US economy started 2026 in a good place, with manufacturing showing greater resilience. Retail sales were up 0.6% month-over-month. Real GDP growth in Q4 2025 was 2.0%, a decrease from 2.4% a year prior, partly attributed to tariffs. Forecasts for real US GDP growth in 2026 average 2.7%, supported by modest population growth and innovation-led productivity.

### Europe
Eurozone GDP growth is noted at 0.8%. No other major developments this week.

## Labour Market
### United States
The US labor market saw a strong rebound, with nonfarm payrolls rising by 178k. JOLTS job openings fell to 6.88 million. Manufacturing employment, however, decreased by 98,000 jobs in the 12 months to February 2026, partly due to tariffs. The unemployment rate is forecast to average 4.4% in 2026.

### Europe
No major developments this week.

## Central Banks & Interest Rates
### Federal Reserve
With inflation uncertainty rising, the market is now pricing in no Fed rate cuts for the remainder of 2026, a shift from the two cuts anticipated before recent geopolitical events. Fed officials have also suggested that stronger AI-driven productivity could elevate the neutral short-term interest rate (r*), potentially constraining the central bank's ability to ease policy even if the labor market weakens. The Fed Funds rate is forecast to end 2026 at 3.13%.

### European Central Bank
ECB official Fabio Panetta indicated that the impact of the war would persist, affecting inflation. The 3-month EURIBOR rate is currently at 2.10%.

### Bank of England
No major developments this week.

## Sell-Side Research Summary

**Goldman Sachs:** No major developments this week

**JPMorgan Asset Management:** JPMorgan highlights that the U.S./Iran conflict led to a challenging month for stocks and bonds in March, with the 60/40 portfolio declining 3.7% due to inflation fears from rising energy prices. They expect volatility to persist given heightened geopolitical tensions and suggest inflation-hedging alternatives like private infrastructure or real estate for stable, uncorrelated returns.

**Bank of America:** Bank of America notes that US tariffs have contributed to higher goods inflation, reduced manufacturing employment, and slowed GDP growth in Q4 2025. They emphasize US dependence on critical minerals, largely controlled by China, but remain constructive on US equities, viewing pullbacks as opportunities due to improving growth and limited geopolitical risk impact. They are constructive on Fixed Income but underweight it to fund Equities, expecting yields to stay range-bound amid sticky inflation and GDP near or above 2%.
