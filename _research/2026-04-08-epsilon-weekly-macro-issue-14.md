# Weekly Macro Briefing
## Week ending 10 April 2026

---

## 1. Geopolitics
Geopolitical tensions intensified this week, with Iran pressing attacks across the Persian Gulf and President Trump announcing fresh US attacks. This conflict has led to oil price jumps, a rebound in oil and gas shares, and drops in gold and silver prices, while shares in airlines and cruise operators slipped. JPMorgan notes that the onset of the US/Iran conflict contributed to a challenging month for markets, fueling fears of higher near-term inflation and increasing overall volatility.

Separately, the impact of President Trump's "Liberation Day" tariffs continues to be felt, with JPMorgan highlighting that these tariffs have increased US goods inflation and weighed on growth, causing manufacturing employment to fall and GDP growth to slow in Q4 2025. Policy uncertainty surrounding future tariff rates and ongoing trade tensions is expected to prolong inflation volatility.

Bank of America emphasizes the US's persistent dependence on critical minerals, many of which are controlled by China. The latest Middle East conflict, alongside the need to rearm and rebuild global defense bases, is expected to drive higher demand for these minerals, leading to increased protectionism and efforts to reshore supply chains globally.

## 2. Inflation
### United States
Headline CPI for March registered 2.4% year-over-year, with Core CPI at 2.5% year-over-year. Geopolitical tensions and rising energy prices, particularly oil, are fueling fears of higher near-term inflation, with some comparisons to 2022. JPMorgan notes that tariffs have already contributed to goods inflation, which rose to 1.3% in February 2026. Bank of America forecasts 2026 CPI at 3.4% and Core CPI at 2.6%, indicating sticky inflation, but also suggests that AI's impact is more likely to reinforce disinflationary forces due to demand constraints.

### Europe
Eurozone CPI is estimated at 2.5%, and UK CPI at 3.0%. European Central Bank official Fabio Panetta indicated that the impact of war will persist, suggesting ongoing vigilance regarding inflationary pressures. No specific energy price pass-through or second-round effects in wages or services were explicitly detailed this week.

## 3. Growth
### United States
The US economy started 2026 on a positive note, with manufacturing showing greater resilience. Retail sales were up 0.6% month-over-month, and the ISM non-manufacturing PMI for March registered 56.1. Real GDP growth in Q4 2025 was 2.0%, a decline from 2.4% a year prior, partly attributed to the impact of tariffs. Bank of America forecasts 2026 US GDP growth at 2.7% and Q1 2026 at 3.3%, expecting GDP to remain near or above 2%. They also discuss AI's potential to boost productivity and economic capacity, though its realization may be tempered by demand dynamics.

### Europe
Eurozone retail sales for February were up 2.0% year-over-year. Eurozone GDP growth is estimated at 0.8%, and UK GDP growth at 0.2%. No further specific country-level GDP, PMIs, industrial production, or sentiment indicators were reported this week.

## 4. Labour Market
### United States
Nonfarm payrolls rose by 178k, indicating a strong rebound after winter strikes and storms. JOLTS job openings fell to 6.88 million. Manufacturing employment, however, saw a decline of 98,000 jobs over the past year, partly due to tariffs. Bank of America forecasts the unemployment rate for 2026 at 4.4% and discusses how AI could potentially lead to higher structural unemployment, though tempered by demand. US net immigration in 2025 was 1.3 million, a sharp fall from 2.7 million in 2024 but still above the 35-year average, which supports labor force growth.

### Europe
No major developments this week.

## 5. Central Banks & Interest Rates
### Federal Reserve
Market expectations for Fed policy have shifted significantly, with no rate cuts now priced in for the remainder of 2026, a change from two cuts expected before recent geopolitical tensions. Fed officials have suggested that AI-driven productivity could push the neutral short-term interest rate (r*) higher, potentially limiting the Fed's ability to ease policy if the labor market weakens. However, current policy projections and market pricing do not indicate a material rise in equilibrium rates. Bank of America forecasts the Fed Funds rate to end 2026 at 3.13%. The US 2-year Treasury yield is 3.84%, and the 10-year Treasury yield is 4.35%.

### European Central Bank
ECB official Fabio Panetta indicated that the impact of war will persist, suggesting ongoing vigilance regarding monetary policy. The 10-year German Bund yield is 3.00%, and the 3-month EURIBOR is 2.10%. No explicit decisions or forward guidance were provided by the ECB this week.

### Bank of England
The UK 10-year Gilt yield is 4.84%. No major decisions, speeches, or forward guidance from the Bank of England were reported this week.

## 7. Sell-Side Research Summary
**Goldman Sachs:** No major developments this week.

**JPMorgan Asset Management:** JPMorgan highlights that the US/Iran conflict led to a challenging month for markets, with fears of higher inflation pushing yields up and equity markets down, resulting in a significant decline for the 60/40 portfolio in March. They note that markets are now pricing in no Fed rate cuts for 2026 and suggest that geopolitical tensions will sustain volatility, recommending inflation-hedging alternatives.

**Bank of America:** Bank of America argues that AI's impact will likely reinforce disinflationary forces rather than driving higher interest rates, with productivity growth remaining moderate. They emphasize US dependence on critical minerals controlled by China, foreseeing increased protectionism and opportunities in "picks and shovels" companies. BofA also views US demographics as a favorable outlier due to continued population growth, supporting long-run economic growth and market returns.
