## 1. Geopolitics
Geopolitical tensions in the Middle East intensified this week, with Iran pressing on with attacks across the Persian Gulf and President Trump announcing plans for fresh US attacks. This conflict has significantly impacted markets, contributing to a challenging month for both stocks and bonds. Fears over higher near-term inflation, stemming from increased energy prices, have sent equity markets lower and yields higher.

Saudi Arabia raised oil prices, and oil prices jumped following Trump's speech, leading to a rebound in shares of oil and gas companies. Conversely, gold and silver prices dropped. The ongoing conflict is expected to add to demand for rearming and rebuilding global defense and industrial bases. While the US is energy-independent in oil and natural gas, it remains highly dependent on critical minerals, with China maintaining a chokehold on their refining output, posing a national security and economic competitiveness concern.

## 2. Inflation
### United States
Fears over higher near-term inflation, stemming from increased energy prices due to the US/Iran conflict, have pushed inflation uncertainty to the forefront. The market is now pricing in no rate cuts for the remainder of 2026, a shift from the two cuts anticipated before the conflict. However, any upward pressure on inflation is expected to be short-lived, assuming the conflict does not drag on. Goods inflation rose from 0.5% to 1.3% year-on-year in the 12 months to February 2026, partly due to tariffs. Headline CPI for March was 2.4% y/y, with Core CPI at 2.5% y/y, and Core PCE for February at 3.1% y/y. Forecasts suggest CPI inflation at 3.4% for 2026 and Core CPI at 2.6% for 2026.

### Europe
ECB official Fabio Panetta noted that the impact of the war will persist. Italian inflation saw a modest increase but substantial risks remain. Eurozone CPI is forecast at 2.5% for 2026, and UK CPI at 3.0% for 2026. Energy price pass-through and second-round effects remain a concern given the geopolitical developments.

## 3. Growth
### United States
The US economy started 2026 in a good place, with manufacturing showing greater resilience. Retail sales were up 0.6% month-over-month. The ISM non-manufacturing PMI for March was 56.1. Real US GDP growth is projected at 2.7% for 2026, with Q1 2026 annualized growth at 3.3%. Despite tariffs impacting goods inflation and manufacturing employment, the US economy continues to demonstrate resilience, with GDP expected to remain near or above 2%.

### Europe
Eurozone retail sales for February were up 2.0% year-on-year. Real GDP growth for the Eurozone is projected at 0.8% for 2026, and for the UK at 0.2% for 2026.

---

## 4. Labour Market
### United States
The US labor market saw a strong rebound after winter strikes and storms. Nonfarm payrolls rose by 178k, while JOLTS job openings fell to 6.88mn. The unemployment rate is forecast to be 4.4% for 2026. Manufacturing employment fell by 98,000 jobs over the past year. Net immigration in the US fell sharply by nearly 54% in 2025 to 1.3 million people, down from 2.7 million in 2024, but this figure was still ahead of the 35-year average of 964,000, suggesting a reversion to the mean after artificially inflated flows from 2022-2024. The US continues to post positive population growth, with over 500,000 more births than deaths last year, supporting labor force growth, consumption, and innovation capacity.

### Europe
No major developments this week.

## 5. Central Banks & Interest Rates
### Federal Reserve
The market is now pricing in no rate cuts for the remainder of 2026, a shift from previous expectations, primarily due to inflation uncertainty stemming from geopolitical tensions. Fed officials have suggested that stronger AI-driven productivity could push the neutral short-term interest rate (r*) higher, potentially limiting the Fed's ability to ease policy. However, the prevailing view is that AI's impact is more likely to reinforce disinflationary forces rather than structurally higher interest rates. The Fed Funds rate is forecast to end 2026 at 3.13%.

### European Central Bank
ECB official Fabio Panetta indicated that the impact of the war will persist, suggesting ongoing vigilance regarding economic conditions. The 3-month EURIBOR stood at 2.10% as of April 3, 2026.

### Bank of England
The UK 10Y Gilt Yield was 4.84% as of March 4, 2026, experiencing a week-on-week change of -0.13%.

## 7. Sell-Side Research Summary

**Goldman Sachs:** No major developments this week

**JPMorgan Asset Management:** The onset of the U.S./Iran conflict led to a very challenging month for both stocks and bonds, with fears over higher near-term inflation from increased energy prices sending equity markets lower and yields higher. Volatility is likely to persist as geopolitical tensions remain heightened, and adding inflation-hedging alternatives to portfolios can provide stable, uncorrelated returns.

**Bank of America:** US consumers have felt the impact of President Trump's Liberation Day tariffs, contributing to increased goods inflation and slower GDP growth. The US is not truly energy independent due to its reliance on critical minerals from China, which poses a national security concern. Despite a recent fall in net immigration, the US remains a favorable outlier among developed nations with positive population growth, supporting long-run economic growth and market returns, and AI's impact is expected to reinforce disinflationary forces rather than structurally higher interest rates.
