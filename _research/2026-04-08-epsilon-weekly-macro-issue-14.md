# Weekly Macro Briefing
## Week ending 10 April 2026

---

## 1. Geopolitics
The US/Iran conflict continues to be a key geopolitical driver, with Iran pressing on with attacks across the Persian Gulf and President Trump announcing plans for fresh US attacks. This has led to oil prices jumping and Saudi Arabia raising prices, contributing to fears of higher near-term inflation and sending equity markets lower while yields rose. The prospect of further attacks also impacted airline and cruise operator shares negatively, while oil and gas companies rebounded.

Beyond the immediate conflict, geopolitical tensions are also shaping trade and supply chain policies. The US is increasingly focused on critical minerals, where China maintains a significant chokehold on refining and supply. The latest Middle East conflict is expected to further drive demand to rearm and rebuild global defense and industrial bases, highlighting the strategic importance of these minerals. Efforts towards protectionism and reshoring supply chains for critical resources are expected to be key themes globally.

---

## 2. Inflation
### United States
Inflation concerns are resurfacing, with some comparisons to 2022 dynamics. Fears of higher near-term inflation, driven by increased energy prices due to geopolitical tensions, have pushed equity markets lower and yields higher. The market is now pricing in no rate cuts for the remainder of 2026, a shift from previous expectations of two cuts before the conflict began.

Looking ahead, the US is anticipating the release of March Headline and Core CPI data this week, with forecasts at 2.4% and 2.5% year-on-year respectively. Tariffs are also noted to have contributed to goods inflation, rising from 0.5% to 1.3% in the 12 months to February 2026, suggesting that higher inflation volatility is likely to persist. Bank of America forecasts sticky inflation, with CPI expected to average 3.4% in 2026.

### Europe
Eurozone HICP (CPI) was reported at 2.5% for the week of 03/04. ECB official Fabio Panetta indicated that the impact of the ongoing conflict would persist, suggesting continued inflationary pressures. While Italian inflation saw a modest increase, substantial risks remain.

---

## 3. Growth
### United States
The US economy shows signs of resilience, with the ISM non-manufacturing PMI for March expected at 56.1, suggesting a good start to 2026. Manufacturing is also demonstrating greater resilience. Retail sales increased by 0.6% month-over-month. However, real GDP growth in Q4 2025 was 2.0%, a deceleration from 2.4% a year prior, with tariffs noted to have weighed on growth. Bank of America forecasts US GDP to average 2.7% in 2026.

### Europe
Eurozone GDP growth was reported at 0.8% for the week of 03/04, while UK GDP growth was 0.2%. Retail sales for the Eurozone in February are expected to be released this week, with a prior estimate of 2.0% year-on-year.

---

## 4. Labour Market
### United States
The US labor market saw a rebound, with nonfarm payrolls increasing by 178,000. However, JOLTS job openings fell to 6.88 million, and manufacturing employment declined by 98,000 over the past year, partly attributed to tariffs. The unemployment rate is projected to remain stable, averaging 4.4% in 2026.

Net immigration in the US decreased significantly last year, though the 1.3 million new immigrants still exceeded the 35-year average. This normalization follows a period of artificially inflated migrant flows due to prior loose immigration policies. The US continues to experience positive population growth, which supports labor force growth and consumption, contrasting with aging and shrinking populations in other developed nations.

### Europe
No major developments this week.

---

## 5. Central Banks & Interest Rates
### Federal Reserve
Market expectations for Fed policy have shifted significantly, with no rate cuts now priced in for the remainder of 2026, a change from the two cuts anticipated before the recent geopolitical conflict. This shift is driven by renewed inflation uncertainty. Fed officials have also noted that strong AI-driven productivity could potentially raise the neutral short-term interest rate (r*), which might limit the Fed's ability to ease policy even if the labor market weakens. The Fed Funds rate is projected to end 2026 at 3.13%.

### European Central Bank
ECB official Fabio Panetta commented that the impact of the ongoing conflict would persist, suggesting continued vigilance on monetary policy. The 3-month EURIBOR stands at 2.10%, and the 10-year German Bund yield is at 3.00%.

### Bank of England
No major developments this week. The UK 10-year Gilt yield is currently at 4.84%.

## 7. Sell-Side Research Summary
**Goldman Sachs:** No major developments this week.

**JPMorgan Asset Management:** JPMorgan notes that the US/Iran conflict made March a challenging month for both stocks and bonds, with fears of higher near-term inflation from increased energy prices driving markets lower and yields higher. The 60/40 portfolio saw its second-worst monthly decline since September 2022. They anticipate persistent volatility due to geopolitical tensions and suggest adding inflation-hedging alternatives like private infrastructure or real estate for stable, uncorrelated returns.

**Bank of America:** Bank of America views equity market pullbacks from headline noise as potential opportunities, supported by improving growth, clearer interest-rate visibility, and strong earnings. They emphasize diversification beyond US mega-caps, recommending exposure to Small-caps, Emerging Markets, and selective sector shifts. BofA remains constructive on Fixed Income but underweights it to fund Equities, expecting tariffs to have a marginal economic impact and yields to stay range-bound amid sticky inflation and GDP near or above 2%. They also favor the US over other regions due to modest population growth and innovation-led productivity.
